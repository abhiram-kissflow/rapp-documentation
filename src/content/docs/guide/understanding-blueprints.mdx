After you describe your app and answer the AI's questions, RAPP generates a **blueprint** — a complete, structured plan for your application. This page explains what's in a blueprint and how to navigate it.

## What is a blueprint?

A blueprint is your app's master plan. It's a detailed specification that covers everything about your application: what it does, who uses it, how workflows operate, what data gets collected, what rules govern behavior, and what external systems it connects to.

Think of it like an architect's blueprint for a building. Before any construction starts, the blueprint lays out every room, door, hallway, and utility connection. RAPP's blueprints do the same thing for your app.

The AI generates your blueprint based on your description and interview answers. You can review it, refine it, and update it at any time through the Builder.

## The six sections

Every blueprint is organized into six sections. Together, they describe your entire application.

### Overview

The overview is a summary of what your app does. It includes the app's name, its purpose, and a high-level description of its key features. This is the "elevator pitch" for your application.

### Roles

Roles define **who uses your app** and what they can do. A leave management app might have three roles:

- **Employee** — Submits leave requests and views their own history
- **Manager** — Reviews and approves or rejects requests from their team
- **HR Admin** — Manages policies, views all records, generates reports

Each role has a name, a description, and a set of capabilities. Roles control what each user sees and what actions they can take.

### Processes

Processes describe **step-by-step workflows** — the sequences of actions that happen in your app. For example, a leave request process might look like this:

1. Employee fills out the leave request form
2. Manager receives a notification and reviews the request
3. Manager approves or rejects the request
4. Employee is notified of the decision
5. If approved, the leave is recorded in the system

Each process has a name, a list of steps, and information about who is responsible for each step.

### Forms

Forms define **the screens where users enter data**. Each form includes a list of fields — things like text inputs, date pickers, dropdowns, and checkboxes. The blueprint specifies:

- The form's name and purpose
- Each field's label, type, and whether it's required
- Validation rules (for example, "end date must be after start date")

### Rules

Rules capture the **business logic** that governs your app. These are the "if this, then that" conditions that make your app behave correctly. Examples:

- "Only managers can approve leave requests"
- "Expenses over $500 require VP approval"
- "Users cannot submit more than 3 requests per week"

Rules help ensure your app enforces the right policies automatically.

### Integrations

Integrations describe **connections to other systems**. Your app might need to send email notifications, sync with a calendar, post messages to Slack, or connect to an existing database. The integrations section lists these connections and what data flows between them.

## The three tabs

When you view a blueprint in the Builder, you'll see three tabs at the top. Each tab offers a different perspective on your app.

<Tabs>
  <TabItem label="What (Spec)">
    This is the main tab. It shows all six sections of your blueprint — overview, roles, processes, forms, rules, and integrations — in a readable, structured format. This is where you'll spend most of your time reviewing and refining.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Design (Flows)">
    This tab shows visual workflow diagrams for your app's processes. Instead of reading steps as a list, you can see them as a flowchart. This is helpful for understanding how different steps connect and where decision points occur.
  </TabItem>
  <TabItem label="Developer (Build)">
    This tab shows the technical output — the generated app code and implementation details. Most users won't need this tab, but it's there if you want to see what's being built under the hood.
  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## How to read your blueprint

When you first see your blueprint, start with the **Overview** to confirm the AI understood your app's purpose. Then check the **Roles** to make sure the right people are represented. Next, walk through each **Process** to verify the workflow steps make sense. Review the **Forms** to see if the right data is being collected, and check the **Rules** to confirm your business logic is captured correctly.

You don't need to memorize every detail. The blueprint is a living document — you can update it anytime by chatting with the AI in the Builder.

<Aside type="tip">
Click on any item in a section to open its details in the inspector panel. This lets you see the full description, properties, and relationships for that item.
</Aside>

## How blueprints evolve

Your blueprint is not set in stone. As you work in the Builder, you can ask the AI to make changes:

- "Add a notification step after approval"
- "Create a new role for department heads"
- "Remove the email integration"
- "Change the expense limit rule from $500 to $1,000"

Each change updates the blueprint in real time. You can also switch between tabs to see how your changes affect the workflow diagrams and technical implementation.

Over time, your blueprint becomes more detailed and refined as you iterate. Think of it as a collaborative document between you and the AI — you provide the direction, and the AI handles the details.
